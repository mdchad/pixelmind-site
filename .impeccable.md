## Design Context

### Users
Founders, product managers, and business owners — typically at early-to-growth stage startups or SMBs — evaluating whether Pixelmind Studio is the right team to build their product. They browse during working hours, often on a desktop, comparing multiple agencies. Their primary job: get enough confidence to reach out. Secondary job: understand what Pixelmind actually does and whether it's relevant to their problem.

SEA-region context (Malaysian/Singapore primary audience). Many potential clients are building apps in sectors with strong community meaning — Islamic tools, local tourism, consumer fintech. The studio needs to feel trustworthy and human, not corporate or cold.

### Brand Personality
**Unhurried. Considered. Craft-focused.**

Not a factory. Not a hustle-culture dev shop. A small team that takes their time and does it properly. The interface should feel like meeting a thoughtful person, not reading a brochure. Confidence through quietness — not through hero numbers and feature lists.

Emotional goal: a visitor should feel *reassured* and *curious*, not overwhelmed or sold to. The goal is to make them want to send an email, not sign a contract.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Soft editorial light mode.** Warm cream surfaces, terracotta accent used sparingly (10% rule), generous whitespace, unhurried layout pacing. Feels like quality print — a boutique architecture firm's portfolio brochure, or a considered stationery brand.

**Anti-references**: No terminal/hacker aesthetic (the old design). No AI-purple gradients. No hero metric layouts (big number / small label grids). No identical 3-column card grids. No glassmorphism. Nothing that screams "SaaS startup template."

**Theme**: Light only. The product is evaluated during working hours, in bright office or café settings. Dark mode adds no value here and would undermine the warmth.

**Typography pairing**:
- Display / headings: **Cardo** (Google Fonts) — warm classical serif, feels like quality print, has craft character without being stuffy. Not on the banned-reflex list.
- Body / UI: **Figtree** (Google Fonts) — humanist sans, friendly and readable without being childish or generic. Not on the banned-reflex list.
- Both loaded via `next/font/google`. Geist Sans (already in project) can be used for small UI labels/metadata where a neutral sans is needed.

**Color system (OKLCH)**:
- Background: `oklch(97% 0.008 68)` — warm cream, tinted toward the terracotta hue
- Surface card: `oklch(99% 0.004 68)` — near-white, very slightly warm
- Soft surface: `oklch(94% 0.012 68)` — deeper cream for section alternation
- Accent (terracotta): `oklch(62% 0.12 48)` — warm, earthy, not neon
- Text primary: `oklch(20% 0.015 60)` — warm near-black
- Text muted: `oklch(52% 0.022 60)` — warm mid-gray, never cold
- Border: `oklch(88% 0.014 68)` — soft, barely-there line

### Design Principles

1. **Quiet confidence over loud claims.** No hero metrics, no "we've served X clients" stat grids. Let the actual work speak. Show projects with care; describe them plainly.

2. **Warmth through restraint.** The terracotta accent earns its weight precisely because it's used rarely — one CTA, one highlight, one decorative touch. Diluting it kills the effect.

3. **Unhurried rhythm.** Sections breathe. Headings have space above them. Nothing is crammed. The pace of scrolling should feel like turning pages, not scanning a dashboard.

4. **Paired typography as personality.** Cardo headings + Figtree body is the visual fingerprint of this brand. Use it consistently. Cardo brings warmth and craft; Figtree brings clarity and approachability. Never substitute either for a generic sans.

5. **Human copy, direct structure.** Sentence case throughout. No corporate buzzwords. No "seamless," "next-gen," or "elevate." Write like a person talking to a person. The structure should be: who we are → what we do → what we've built → get in touch. No detours.
